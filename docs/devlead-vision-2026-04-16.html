A governance layer that makes AI pair-programming accountable. One channel between the codebase and the model — no dark code, no silent slips, no session amnesia.

The problem — deeper than it looks

One sentence: software engineering's biggest unsolved problem is the missing link between what the business is trying to achieve and what the code actually does. We call that link Business Convergence — the gravitational pull every committed line should feel toward a declared business objective. DevLead is the first tool that installs this link as infrastructure rather than leaving it as tribal knowledge.

Layer 1 — The career-defining skill almost nobody has

The unsexy truth about software careers: the engineers who rise, get paid, and end up running teams are not the ones who ship the most code. They are the ones whose code converges on a business objective. They hold the business outcome in their head while they type. They kill scope that doesn't move the metric. They notice when the ticket they were handed won't actually deliver the thing the ticket was written for. They push back on the PM with "this story is green but the number didn't move — we're not done."

That skill has no agreed-upon name. We call it Business Convergence — the formal term, tied directly to weighted convergence math (TTO → TBO → BO). Every committed line contributes a fraction to a rollup percentage against a named business objective.

This skill is the single biggest differentiator in engineering and it is nowhere taught. It's picked up by apprenticeship, accidental trauma, or never at all. The majority of engineers are technically excellent and strategically invisible. Their code ships; the metric doesn't move; they wonder why the promotion went to the other guy. DevLead's ambition is to make this skill the default, not the privilege of the few.

Layer 2 — AI cannot do this, by construction

It's tempting to hope AI pair-programming closes this gap. It does the opposite. An LLM converts an idea held in the prompt window into code — instantly, at zero friction, with no persistent link to the business objective that spawned the idea two months ago. The model has no memory of the BO. It has no mechanism to check its output against the BO. It has no stake in whether the shipped feature moves the metric. It just converts.

Read that again, because it's the load-bearing claim of this document: an AI coding assistant is a point-in-time idea-to-code converter. It is not, and cannot be, a convergence engine. Business Convergence requires persistent state (the objective), a running check (am I still converging?), and the authority to refuse work that doesn't. An autoregressive model running one prompt at a time has none of those by default.

This is not a limitation that scales away with a bigger model. A bigger model converts ideas into code faster — which accelerates the drift. Every session without a governance layer is a tiny further divergence. Six months in, the codebase is a compendium of small-correct decisions that together deliver the wrong product.

Layer 3 — No tool on earth maintains the link

Look at the toolchain honestly:

  • JIRA, Linear, Asana track tickets. A ticket is a unit of work, not a unit of convergence. A green ticket doesn't mean the number moved.
  • Git tracks changes. A diff has no field for "which objective does this serve."
  • Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code, Aider track the session. The session ends; the link to the objective dies with the window.
  • OKR tools (Mooncamp, Lattice) track the objective but have zero awareness of the code. They're a slide deck, not a runtime.

There is a gaping hole in the toolchain where a living link between "what we are trying to achieve as a business" and "what was just committed to main" should live. Nothing maintains that link today. The best engineers maintain it in their heads; everyone else fakes it in standups. DevLead fills the hole.

Layer 4 — AI pair-programming turned this latent problem into an acute one

Before AI pair-programming, the drift was slow enough that a good tech lead could catch it in code review. After AI pair-programming, drift happens at the speed of typing. The AI invents work that was never requested, forgets context between sessions, and produces code that nobody can later trace to a requirement. The product owner ends up managing the AI's hallucinations instead of managing the project. Existing PM tools don't know the AI exists; the AI doesn't know the PM tool exists.

Non-coder product owners feel this worst. They can't verify a diff. They can't tell if the AI built the right thing. They can't prove to themselves that work traces to an intent. Projects pile up, unfinished, in a graveyard of "I thought we were almost there."

What DevLead does about it

DevLead installs Business Convergence as infrastructure, not tribal knowledge. Every unit of work — human-authored or AI-authored — is forced to be declared, placed in a hierarchy, weighted, and rolled up into a live convergence percentage visible on the product owner's dashboard. The brake on Claude Code's Edit/Write tool is not the feature; it's the enforcement mechanism. The feature is that every engineer, and every AI session, now inherits the convergence skill on day one.

The dark-code problem, the session-amnesia problem, and the deadline-slip problem are all symptoms. Business Convergence is the disease. DevLead is the first tool that names it and treats it.

What DevLead actually is

A governance layer, not a framework

DevLead installs onto an existing project with devlead init. It creates devlead_docs/ — the single, canonical source of truth — and wires Claude Code hooks that gate every Edit/Write against an active intake entry.

Markdown-driven core

Everything is plain Markdown: intake, hierarchy, decisions, awareness files, scratchpad, audit log. Git-native. Grep-able. No database to host, no SaaS to trust. The open-source core works fully offline.

Six responsibilities, hard-enforced

  • R1 — Build and maintain the BO → TBO → TTO hierarchy
  • R2 — No coding outside intake context (the discipline rule)
  • R4 — Deliver BOs on time; track slips with justification
  • R5 — Project-management answers computed from data, not vibes
  • R6 — KPIs generated from every DevLead action
  • Session bootstrap — sessions never start from zero

Plugin bridge, not plugin replacement

DevLead doesn't compete with Superpowers, brainstorming, writing-plans, or any other Claude Code plugin. It ingests their output. Run your planning plugin normally, then /devlead ingest <plan> --into _intake_features.md and the work is tracked.

Motivation — why this, why now

  • AI coding exploded in 18 months. Accountability tooling for AI coding did not.
  • Every engineer using Cursor, Claude Code, Copilot, Aider has the same complaint: "I don't remember what we were doing," "the AI went off on a tangent," "I have files in my repo I don't recognize."
  • As AI-generated code enters production, compliance questions arrive: who authorized this change? what requirement does it satisfy? Today there is no answer.
  • The product owner's pain is acute and universal: projects don't finish. DevLead's discipline loop is explicitly designed for the owner who cannot read Python but can read Markdown.

Honest take on the caliber

What is genuinely strong

  • The category doesn't exist yet. "AI governance for AI coding" has no named competitor. Linear/JIRA don't gate the model. Copilot/Cursor don't govern themselves. That gap is the entire opportunity.
  • The discipline loop is a moat. scratchpad → triage → plan → intake → hierarchy → code is a real workflow, not a marketing diagram.
  • Hook enforcement is the "teeth." Most governance tools are advisory. DevLead's PreToolUse gate literally blocks Edit/Write when there's no focus. That's the difference between a policy doc and a working brake.
  • Markdown-first is correct. Git-native, grep-able, no vendor lock-in, works offline, zero SaaS tax.
  • Plugin-bridge, not plugin-replacement. DevLead wins by being the connective tissue, not a rival.

What is still weak

  • v2 is stubs in places. Convergence math + project-init CLI + effort wiring all on the build list — see Tab 5 Phase 1.
  • Discipline tax is real. If the user skips intake, dark code returns. Onboarding needs ruthless defaults — the project-init interview is the answer.
  • Claude-Code coupling. Today DevLead is effectively a Claude Code plugin. Cursor / Aider portability is the #1 distribution risk.
  • Convergence math unvalidated externally. Weighted rollups per BO are clean on paper; nobody outside this repo has stress-tested them. Tab 5 BO-4 makes this the validation thesis.

Net: right product, right moment, by someone with earned pain. Risk is execution, not concept. Tab 5 shows the Phase 1 plan that closes the build gap.

Three deltas Tab 2 must close

  1. From single-tool to multi-tool. Today: Claude Code only. Target: Cursor, Aider, Copilot — same governance, same intake, same convergence math.
  2. From local to networked. Today: devlead_docs/ in one repo on one machine. Target: signed-sync to a cloud read-view; team-shared hierarchies; cross-project convergence rollup.
  3. From self-reported to verified. Today: Claude marks its own TTOs done. Target: every functional TTO carries a verify: shell command; only DevLead checks the box, only on exit-zero. Convergence becomes evidence-backed. Phase 1 ships the foundation for this — see Tab 5 BO-1.

Technical architecture — three concentric rings

Ring A — Local core (open source, Python)

  • src/devlead/ — CLI, hooks runner, ingest/promote/triage logic, gate enforcement, verify runner, convergence math.
  • State: devlead_docs/*.md + _audit_log.jsonl. No DB. Git is the history.
  • Integrations: Claude Code hooks (shipping), Cursor rules (next), Aider commands (after).
  • Extensibility: every command is a Python module + a Markdown slash-command file in commands/. Contributors add routes, not plumbing.

Ring B — Cloud (paid) — convergence at scale

  • Thin API (FastAPI to ship in a week): read-only view over a synced devlead_docs/.
  • Sync mechanism: signed Git push from local to a hosted repo; dashboard renders from it. No proprietary state. User can always leave — the truth is still in their repo.
  • Cross-project convergence rollup: an org sees one number across N repos. KPI history persists beyond the laptop.
  • Hosting: Cloudflare Workers + R2 + Pages. Auth: GitHub OAuth. Billing: Stripe. All deferred until Phase 1 ships.

Ring C — Ecosystem (community)

  • Plugin bridge registry: every supported planning plugin has an ingest adapter contributed by the community.
  • Dashboard themes + exporters (PDF weekly report for execs).

Risks I would not wave away

Product risks

  • Discipline fatigue → churn. Mitigation: ruthless defaults, visible escape hatch.
  • Hook enforcement bugs blocking legit edits. Mitigation: warn-mode default for first 10 sessions, then auto-promote to hard.
  • Convergence math doesn't validate in the wild. Weighted rollups TTO → TBO → BO are clean on paper; they may produce numbers product owners don't trust. Mitigation: Tab 5 BO-4 makes one external user the entire validation thesis of Phase 1.

Adoption risks

  • "Free OSS is enough." Mitigation: Pro tier (Phase 2) leads with team-shared convergence URL — something OSS can't cleanly replicate.
  • Competitor ships first (probable: a Superpowers skill pack). Mitigation: ship faster; that is the only real answer.
  • Convergence framework gets cargo-culted without metrics. Mitigation: refuse to compute C(τ) for BOs without a real metric_source — no fake numbers.

What I trust — and would tell my team to use today

The PreToolUse brake

I have never seen another AI-coding tool put a hook on the model's Edit/Write before this. Real teeth. Even in warn-only it nudges the model. In hard mode (the config supports it; the lock is product-side) it would literally exit-2 the AI's tool call. That is the first authentic governance primitive in this category.

Caveat: it covers Edit/Write/MultiEdit only. The AI can still drift via Bash, MCP tools, and chat-talked-into-it edits. The brake is on one wheel — but one wheel is more brake than anyone else has shipped.

The verifier

This is the feature I want most. AI cannot mark its own TTO done. Only the verifier can — and only if the verify: shell command exits 0. This kills the single most expensive failure mode in AI-coded teams: "claimed done, actually broken." Concrete, small, hard to game.

If the rest of DevLead vanished and I kept just this primitive, I'd still install it.

The audit log

Append-only JSONL, structured schema, 2,100 events accumulated in their own dogfood project. My compliance officer would hug the founder. This is the answer to "show me which AI session authorized which change" — finally.

Auto-generated CLAUDE.md

The next session reads context from one file that DevLead generates from devlead_docs/. I can read it. I can audit it. I can diff it. It's not a magic context window I have to trust — it's a Markdown file in git. That is the right answer to the session-amnesia problem.

/devlead focus

Focus is a status flip on the intake entry. No second source of truth, no session_state.json, no separate cache. One command. Beautifully un-engineered. This is what a tool looks like when the author has the discipline to not add abstractions.

Convergence — the math layer

What this tab is. The math engine that powers Business Convergence — five computed scalars over an intent matrix and a goal vector. The hierarchy these run on, and the project-init flow that drafts that hierarchy, both live in Tab 5. Read this tab only if you want to see how the numbers underneath are computed.

The math, applied

Below: the same five computed scalars from the convergence framework, run on a sample of 10 TTOs drawn from Tab 5's four release-world BOs. Math is the engine; the table headings are what an engineer would actually see on the dashboard.

Goal vector and today's state

g = (0.30, 0.20, 0.30, 0.20) weighted by BO priority (Σwᵢ = 1)
s(today) = (0, 0, 0, 0) per-axis normalised progress, nothing shipped yet
C(today) = s · g = Σ wᵢ × sᵢ = 0.0% converged

Formula correction (vs early Tab-4 drafts): convergence ratio is the weighted progress s · g, which sits in [0, 1] at full convergence when weights sum to 1. The earlier (s·g)/(g·g) form was a scalar projection that overshot 1 at the limit; the corrected form is what convergence.compute_C actually computes (verified by 40 unit tests).

One worked realization (the promise ledger in action)

Three weeks pass. Verifier marks TTO-2.1.1 (Stop hook regenerates _resume.md) done. The promise ledger fires a 7-day re-check.

{ "tto_id": "TTO-2.1.1",
  "promised_contribution": [0, 0.74, 0, 0],
  "verified_done_at": "2026-05-08T11:30Z",
  "metric_at_done": { "BO-2_session_start_seconds": 600 },
  "expected_after":  { "BO-2_session_start_seconds": 200 },
  "realization_window_days": 7 }

Seven days later: actual median session-start time is 240 s (not the promised 200 s). Normalised realised progress on BO-2: (600−240)/(600−60) = 0.667.

α(TTO-2.1.1) = 0.392
φ(TTO-2.1.1) = (r·i) / (i·i) = (0.667 × 0.74) / (0.74 × 0.74) = 0.901
ε(TTO-2.1.1) = (r·g) / (i·g) = 0.901

Diagnosis: work landed almost exactly as predicted (φ = 0.90), pulled on the goal as expected (ε = 0.90), but on a moderately-weighted axis (α = 0.39). Translation: "good work, hit the prediction, on a BO that may deserve a higher weight if this kind of pain matters more than I declared." The framework prompts a re-weighting conversation, not a blame conversation.

Conflict detection (the Gram matrix)

Convergence rate theorem applied to my deadline

Six weeks of effective capacity, mostly solo. ‖c‖ ≈ 0.30 (one full sprint of all-out work could ship 30% of the entire intent matrix). Realistic realisation rate from the worked TTO above: φ̄ = 0.6. Hold-the-line discipline: α_min = 0.4.

Per-sprint convergence closure ≈ ‖c‖ × φ̄ × α_min
≈ 0.30 × 0.6 × 0.4 = 0.072 (7.2% per 6-week sprint)

To take C from 0% to a respectable 80% at this rate: ~11 sprints ≈ 16 months. The math forces an honest planning conversation on day zero: either drop a BO from this sprint, recruit one engineer (doubling capacity), or extend the deadline. I cannot fake convergence.
